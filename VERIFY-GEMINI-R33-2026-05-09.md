## Finding 1: "bridge" — 事实性定义错误，可构成误导
- **词条**: bridge, 定义: "a road over water or a valley"
- **问题**: Bridge is a **structure**, not a road. This is a factual error in a children's dictionary. A child who learns "bridge = a road" will form incorrect mental models. Roads are horizontal ground-level paths; bridges are elevated structures. This conflation could lead to errors in geography and science tests.
- **测试用例**: Multiple-choice: "A bridge is a ___." A) road B) structure C) vehicle. Students taught with this definition will choose A, which is wrong.
- **外部证据**: Merriam-Webster: "a structure carrying a pathway or roadway over a depression or obstacle"; Oxford Learner's: "a structure that is built over a river, road, or railway"; Cambridge: "a structure that is built over a river, road, or railway to allow people and vehicles to cross."

## Finding 2: "tunnel" — 同类错误，tunnel ≠ road
- **词条**: tunnel, 定义: "a road that goes through a mountain"
- **问题**: Same category of error as "bridge". A tunnel is a passage or passageway, not a road. Furthermore, limiting it to "through a mountain" excludes subway tunnels, underwater tunnels, and mine tunnels. Double error: wrong genus (road) + too narrow scope (mountain only).
- **测试用例**: "Is the subway tunnel a tunnel?" Student answers "No, because it doesn't go through a mountain." This directly harms test performance.
- **外部证据**: Merriam-Webster: "a covered passageway; especially: a horizontal passageway through or under an obstruction"; Cambridge: "a long passage under or through the ground."

## Finding 3: "lemon" — 非标准词汇选择
- **词条**: lemon, 定义: "a yellow fruit that tastes sharp and not sweet"
- **问题**: Using "sharp" for taste is unusual in pedagogical dictionaries. All major learner dictionaries use "sour" for lemon's taste. "Sharp" has a polysemous problem for ESL learners — its primary meaning is about edges/points. This creates unnecessary cognitive load.
- **测试用例**: Fill-in-the-blank: "A lemon tastes ___." Expected answer: sour. Student writes "sharp" (from this dictionary) and is marked wrong.
- **外部证据**: Cambridge Learner's Dictionary: "sour juice"; Oxford Learner's: "sour"; Merriam-Webster: "acid fruit." Zero major dictionaries use "sharp" for lemon.

## Finding 4: "vest" — genus error (vest ≠ jacket)
- **词条**: vest, 定义: "a jacket with no sleeves"
- **问题**: A vest is not a jacket. In standard American English, a vest is a garment worn UNDER a jacket (as in a three-piece suit). Calling it a jacket creates a genus error. In British English, "vest" means undershirt, making this even more confusing for an international ESL audience.
- **测试用例**: Show a photo of a man in a three-piece suit. Ask "point to the vest." Student points to the jacket (because definition says "a jacket").
- **外部证据**: Merriam-Webster: "a sleeveless garment for the upper body usually worn over a shirt"; Oxford: "a short piece of clothing with buttons down the front but no sleeves, usually worn over a shirt."

## Finding 5: "cottage" — subjective adjective in definition
- **词条**: cottage, 定义: "a small pretty house"
- **问题**: "Pretty" is a subjective aesthetic judgment with no place in a lexicographic definition. It introduces evaluative bias. A dilapidated cottage is still a cottage. No reputable dictionary uses "pretty" in its definition of cottage.
- **测试用例**: Show a run-down cottage image. Student says "that can't be a cottage because cottages are pretty." Definition actively misleads.
- **外部证据**: Merriam-Webster: "the dwelling of a farm laborer or small farmer"; Cambridge: "a small house, usually in the countryside"; Oxford: "a small house, especially in the country." None use "pretty."

## Finding 6: "harbor" — spelling inconsistency check
- **词条**: harbor, 定义: "a safe place where boats park"
- **问题**: The word "park" is anthropomorphic and technically inaccurate for boats. Boats **dock**, **moor**, or **anchor** — they don't "park." While this is a simplification for L1, it teaches an incorrect collocation that a student will have to unlearn.
- **测试用例**: Student writes "The ship parked at the harbor" in an essay. Teacher marks "parked" as incorrect usage. The dictionary taught a bad collocation.
- **外部证据**: Merriam-Webster: "a place of security and comfort"; COCA corpus: "parked" collocates with cars/vehicles, not boats. "Moored/docked at the harbor" is the standard collocation.

## Finding 7: "heel" — definition oversimplified
- **词条**: heel, 定义: "the back part of your foot"
- **问题**: The heel is specifically the **bottom back** part of the foot. "Back part" alone could refer to the Achilles tendon area. More importantly, "heel" also commonly refers to the heel of a shoe, which is arguably the more frequent usage context for children. Definition is anatomically imprecise.
- **测试用例**: Ask student to point to the heel. Student points to the back of the ankle (Achilles area) rather than the bottom. The definition doesn't distinguish.
- **外部证据**: Merriam-Webster: "the back of the human foot below the ankle and behind the arch"; Cambridge: "the rounded back part of the foot." MW specifies "below the ankle" for precision.

## Finding 8: "spine" — definition misses key function
- **词条**: spine, 定义: "the bones down your back that help you stand straight"
- **问题**: The primary function of the spine is to protect the spinal cord and support the body — not just "help you stand straight." Quadrupedal animals have spines too but don't "stand straight." The definition implies spine = posture aid, which is misleading.
- **测试用例**: "Do dogs have spines?" Student answers "No, because dogs don't stand straight." Definition implies spine is only for upright posture.
- **外部证据**: Merriam-Webster: "the spinal column"; Cambridge: "the line of bones down the centre of the back that provides support for the body." Note Cambridge says "support for the body," not "stand straight."
