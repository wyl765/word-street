## Level 2B Issues

### Definition Accuracy

1. **"carbon"** — "a material found in all living things" is reductive. Carbon is a chemical element, not a "material." Definition should say "element" at minimum. The example mentions coal but the definition doesn't connect.

2. **"dinosaur"** — "a very large animal" — not all dinosaurs were large. Many were chicken-sized. Should say "an animal that lived millions of years ago" without the size qualifier.

3. **"tax"** — "money you pay to the state" — oversimplified and US-centric problem: taxes go to federal/local/state governments, not just "the state." Also confusing because "the state" could mean a US state vs government in general. For a 10-year-old ESL learner this ambiguity is real.

4. **"civil"** — "about citizens and their rights" — this is only one meaning. The word also means "polite" (civil conversation). Defining it only as the political sense is misleading when Mark encounters "Be civil" in reading.

5. **"regroup"** — "to trade ten ones for one ten, or ten tens for one hundred" — this is only the math sense. The general English meaning (to reorganize after a setback) is more common in reading. Risky to lock a 10-year-old into thinking this word is math-only.

6. **"end"** — "the last part of a story" — far too narrow. "End" is one of the most common English words; defining it only in story context is bizarre. Mark will be confused when he sees "end of the road."

7. **"table"** — "a chart with rows and columns that organizes facts" — again, only the data sense. The furniture meaning is more primary. If intentionally math-vocabulary, this should be labeled.

8. **"mean"** — "not nice to others on purpose" — only the adjective sense. "Mean" as verb (to signify) is equally critical for a 10-year-old reader. Same single-sense problem.

9. **"carry"** — "to move extra to the next column when adding" — math-only definition. General meaning (to hold and transport) is far more common.

10. **"even"** — "a number that can be split into two equal groups" — math-only. The common adjective meaning (flat, level, equal) is missing.

### Example Sentences

11. **"demanding"** — "The demanding buyer kept asking for changes to her order" — "buyer" is odd word choice for a 10-year-old boy. A "demanding coach" or "demanding teacher" is more relatable for Mark.

12. **"end up"** — "to at last be in a place or case" — the definition uses "case" where it should say "situation." "Case" here is awkward/wrong.

13. **"go through"** — "to feel something, mainly something hard" — definition is garbled. Should be "to experience something, especially something difficult."

14. **"look up"** — imageKeyword "flashlight searching dark room" — searching with a flashlight has nothing to do with looking up a word. Should be "dictionary" or "searching book."

15. **"drop off"** — imageKeyword "mailman delivering package door" — overly specific multi-word imageKeyword; should be "delivering package" or "dropping off."

16. **"make out"** — "to see or get something with trouble" — "get" is wrong here; should be "understand." "Make out" = perceive/discern with difficulty. Definition is inaccurate.

17. **"regroup"** — imageKeyword "kids trading lunch snacks" — completely off-topic for either the math or general meaning.

### imageKeyword Issues

18. **"vowel"** — imageKeyword "aeiou" — not a visual concept; what image would this produce? Should be "vowel letters colorful" or similar.

19. **"consonant"** — imageKeyword "alphabet letter" — too generic, could return any letter including vowels.

20. **"moreover"** — imageKeyword "in addition" — text, not an image concept. Same for "nevertheless" → "still", "nonetheless" → "despite", "accordingly" → "matching", "certainly" → "sure". These abstract transition words all have non-visual imageKeywords that will produce garbage images.

21. **"at the same time"** — imageKeyword "friends holding hands circle" — doesn't convey simultaneity. A split-screen or juggling image would be better.

22. **"especially"** — imageKeyword "child pointing excitedly at one specific ice cream flavor" — way too long for an image search keyword. Trim to "pointing at favorite."

23. **"rather"** — imageKeyword "child choosing a book over watching TV" — same problem, too long.

24. **"in conclusion"** — imageKeyword "book closing last page" — overlong.

### Age Appropriateness / Cognitive Load

25. **"chromosome"** — "a tiny thread in cells that carries body instructions" — this is a legitimate L2 science word but "body instructions" is vague. Should say "information about how your body grows and looks."

26. **"extricate"** — "to free from a tangled case" — "case" should be "situation." Also, "extricate" is a high-register word (SAT-level). Is this appropriate for MAP 197? Borderline.

27. **"brackish"** — very niche vocabulary. A 10-year-old at MAP 197 will almost never encounter this in grade-level reading. Questionable inclusion.

28. **"verdant"** — literary/poetic register. Same concern as brackish.

29. **"aperture"** — highly technical (photography). A 10-year-old ESL learner has near-zero chance of encountering this. Misplaced in L2.

30. **"brunt"** — low-frequency word, almost always in the idiom "bear the brunt." Isolated definition is fine but example sentence uses "bore the brunt" — Mark may not know "bore" (past tense of "bear" in this sense).

31. **"eddy"** — extremely low frequency for a 10-year-old reader.

32. **"cellulose"** — science-specific, unlikely to appear in MAP 197 reading.

### Grammar / Wording

33. **"diet"** — "the food a person or animal most times eats" — should be "usually eats" or "normally eats." "Most times" is not standard English.

34. **"vitamin"** — "a tiny thing in food" — "thing" is too vague. "Substance" or "nutrient" would be more accurate while still being accessible.

35. **"fiber"** — "a part of food that helps your body digest well" — should be "helps your body digest food well" or "helps with digestion."

36. **"revolution"** — example "The revolution began when colonists fought for their freedom from the king" — US-centric. For a Chinese student, a more universal example or at least saying "American colonists" would provide necessary context.

37. **"slavery"** — imageKeyword "chains broken" — this is the imageKeyword for *freedom from* slavery, not slavery itself. Conceptually inverted.

38. **"accommodate"** — "to make room for or help someone" — the "help" sense is weak. Better: "to have enough space for" with the current example.

## Level 2C Issues

### Definition Accuracy

39. **"fault"** — "job for something that went wrong" — **WRONG.** This should be "responsibility for something that went wrong." "Job" makes no sense here. Clear error.

40. **"license"** — "official okay to act" — too informal and vague. Should be "an official paper or card that says you are allowed to do something."

41. **"imply"** — "to suggest something without saying it right at" — **garbled.** "Right at" should be "directly" or "openly." Truncated/broken sentence.

42. **"forecast"** — definition says "a guess about future weather" but example says "The financial forecast predicted that tech company stocks would rise next year." — **definition/example mismatch.** The example is about financial forecasting, not weather. Also "tech company stocks" is not age-appropriate for a 10-year-old.

43. **"function"** — definition says "the job or purpose something has" but example says "In coding, a function is a block of code you can call whenever you need it." — **definition/example mismatch.** The definition is about general function; the example introduces a completely different (programming) meaning.

44. **"logic"** — definition is "thinking in a way that makes sense step by step" (fine) but example says "He used logic to trace through each line and find where the bug was hiding." — **coding-specific example** that doesn't match the general definition. A 10-year-old ESL student will not understand "trace through each line" or "bug" in the programming sense.

45. **"detect"** — example "Smart cameras can detect when someone is at the front door and send an alert." — technology-specific. A simpler, more universal example would serve Mark better.

46. **"evolve"** — example "Voice assistants continue to evolve, getting better at understanding what people mean." — tech-specific example for a biology-origin word. Mismatch in register.

47. **"exploit"** — "to use something in a clever or unfair way" — this conflates two distinct senses (resourceful use vs. taking advantage). The example only shows the negative sense. Misleading.

48. **"complex"** — example "The program was complex, with hundreds of lines of code working together." — another coding example. Pattern of tech-biased examples inappropriate for target user.

49. **"literal"** — "meaning exactly what the words say" — fine, but the example uses "raining cats and dogs" which is actually demonstrating *figurative* language (and saying it's NOT literal). The example teaches by negation, which is cognitively harder for ESL learners.

50. **"novel"** — "new and unlike from anything before" — **grammar error.** Should be "different from" not "unlike from." "Unlike" doesn't take "from."

### Example Sentences

51. **"concern"** — "Dad's concern about the storm made his check the windows twice." — **typo: "his" should be "him."**

52. **"consist"** — "The salad consists of lettuce tomatoes, and carrots." — **missing comma** after "lettuce." Should be "lettuce, tomatoes, and carrots."

53. **"circumstance"** — "Under the circumstances of heavy rain, the teacher called off the field trip." — awkward phrasing. Better: "Because of the circumstances—heavy rain and wind—the teacher canceled the trip."

54. **"fatal"** — "causing death or very serious harm" — "very serious harm" waters down the definition. Fatal means causing death, period. The softened version is inaccurate.

55. **"duty"** — "something you must do because it is your job or job" — **duplicate word "job."** Should be "your job or responsibility."

56. **"immediate"** — "happening right now with no any delay" — **grammar error.** Should be "with no delay" or "without any delay."

57. **"impress"** — "to make someone think you are perfect" — **wrong definition.** Impress means to make someone admire you or think highly of you, not think you are "perfect."

### imageKeyword Issues

58. **"mental"** — imageKeyword "glowing brain with gears" — overly specific, robotic feel for a human concept.

59. **"military"** — imageKeyword "fighter jets in formation" — fine technically but glorifies military hardware for a 10-year-old. "Soldiers saluting" would be more neutral.

### Age Appropriateness

60. **"fatal"** — death-related content. The definition and example ("snakebite can be fatal if you do not get help quickly") are anxiety-inducing for some 10-year-olds. Not necessarily wrong but flagging.

61. **"desperate"** — "The thirsty hiker was desperate to find water" — combined with "fatal" and other intensity words, the emotional load in this level is notable.

62. **"exploit"** — negative social concept ("unfair way") — fine for age but imageKeyword "unfair shortcut" could produce unrelated images.

## Level 2D Issues

### Definition Accuracy

63. **"prospect"** — "the chance of something good happening in the future" — not always good. Prospect is neutral (can be good or bad). "The prospect of rain worried the farmers."

64. **"rebel"** — example "The teenager rebelled by wearing mismatched socks to school" — trivializes rebellion. This is more "being quirky" than rebelling. Definition-example mismatch in severity.

65. **"warrant"** — "to be a good enough reason for something" — this is one meaning, but "warrant" as a noun (legal document) is equally common. Single-sense risk.

66. **"yield"** — "to give way or produce something" — two very different meanings crammed into one definition without distinction. Mark will be confused about which meaning applies when.

67. **"stock"** — "a supply of things kept for future use" — only the inventory sense. Stock as in shares/equity is missing (relevant given the financial content appearing in other entries).

### Example Sentences

68. **"prospect"** — "The prospect of summer trip made the students excited." — **missing article: "a summer trip" or "the summer trip."**

69. **"retire"** — "Grandpa retired from his job, and now spends time gardening." — unnecessary comma before "and" (not a compound sentence with two independent clauses sharing the same subject).

70. **"sequence"** — example "The computer runs each command in sequence, one line after another from top to bottom." — coding-specific again. Same tech-bias pattern from 2C.

71. **"simulate"** — example "The computer can simulate a real basketball game to test different strategies." — another tech example. Definition is general but example is computing.

72. **"remarkable"** — example "It is remarkable that a computer can now write essays and draw pictures." — AI-specific. For a 10-year-old ESL learner, a more tangible example would work better.

73. **"virtual"** — example "The virtual assistant answered every question without needing a real person." — another tech/AI example. The pattern of tech-forward examples is consistent but inappropriate for the target user profile.

74. **"strategy"** — example "Coach drew up a strategy on the whiteboard to beat their zone defense." — "zone defense" is basketball jargon. Mark (Chinese ESL) may not know this term. Adds unnecessary cognitive load.

75. **"anticipate"** — example "A great point guard can anticipate where the pass is going before it's thrown." — "point guard" is basketball-specific. Same jargon problem.

### imageKeyword Issues

76. **"passion"** — imageKeyword "passionate artist" — contains the target word. Image search may just return the word itself rather than a useful image.

77. **"passive"** — imageKeyword "watching cat" — decent but could also return a cat watching TV meme. "Lazy cat watching" might be more specific.

### Grammar / Wording

78. **"plea"** — "a serious and full of feeling request" — **awkward phrasing.** Should be "a serious, emotional request" or "a heartfelt request."

79. **"bankrupt"** — "no one bought something" — **should be "no one bought anything."** Grammar error.

80. **"cumulative"** — "growing by adding more and more overtime" — **"overtime" should be "over time" (two words).** "Overtime" means extra work hours. Different word entirely.

81. **"compulsory"** — "required and must be done" — "required" and "must be done" are the same thing. Redundant definition.

82. **"contempt"** — example "He showed contempt for the easy puzzle by finishing it in seconds." — this doesn't really demonstrate contempt. Finishing quickly shows competence, not disdain. Poor example.

83. **"abolish"** — example uses "let students wear what they want" — this makes "abolish" sound casual/positive. The word typically appears in serious contexts (abolish slavery, abolish a law). The lightweight example doesn't prepare Mark for real reading encounters.

### Age Appropriateness

84. **"corrupt"** — "dishonest and willing to break rules for gain" — example "The corrupt official took money that belonged to the town." Fine for age but heavy topic.

85. **"condemn"** — "to say strongly that something is wrong" — appropriate but combined with corrupt, contempt, betray, this level has a cluster of negative social concepts. Monitor emotional load.

86. **"custody"** — "the right and duty to take care of someone" — the example uses zookeeper (safe), but the word's primary real-world association (custody battles, legal custody) could surface confusing or distressing content for a child of divorced/separated parents. Flagging, not necessarily removing.

## Cross-Cutting Issues (All Three Files)

### Tech-Biased Examples Pattern
Multiple entries across 2C and 2D use coding/tech/AI examples for general vocabulary words: **complex, function, logic, sequence, simulate, remarkable, virtual, detect, evolve, forecast.** This is a systematic bias — likely reflecting the author's background, not the target user's reading context. A 10-year-old Chinese ESL boy at MAP 197 is reading narrative fiction and grade-level nonfiction, not code documentation.

**Recommendation:** Replace all tech/coding examples with grade-level reading contexts.

### Abstract Transition Words (2B) Have Unsearchable imageKeywords
~20 entries for transition words (moreover, nevertheless, consequently, etc.) have imageKeywords that are either other abstract words or short phrases that won't produce meaningful images. These need visual metaphors or should be flagged as "no image."

### Multi-Sense Words Defined with Single Sense
Recurring problem: **end, table, carry, even, mean, civil, warrant, yield, stock, regroup.** When Mark encounters the other sense in reading, these definitions will actively mislead him.

### Inconsistent Definition Register
Some definitions use precise language ("an organ that pumps blood") while others use vague hedging ("a tiny thing in food"). The vague ones should be tightened.
