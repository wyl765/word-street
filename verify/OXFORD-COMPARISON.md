# Oxford Learner's Dictionary Comparison

## Summary
- Words compared: 100 (20 per level across 5 levels)
- Our def matches Oxford primary meaning: 91
- Our def factually diverges from Oxford: 5
- Our def captures secondary/wrong sense: 4

## Methodology
Sampled 20 words evenly from each of: words-level1.js, words-level2.js, words-level3a.js, words-level4a.js, words-level5a.js. Fetched definitions from oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com and compared primary senses.

## Divergences (our def differs from Oxford)

| word | our_def | oxford_def | issue |
|------|---------|------------|-------|
| profound | "very deep and having a lot of meaning" | "very great; felt or experienced very strongly" (primary); "showing great knowledge or understanding" (secondary) | Our def captures secondary sense. Oxford's PRIMARY meaning is "very great/strongly felt" (profound effect, profound shock), not philosophical depth. |
| incline | "a slope; a surface that goes up or down" | Verb: "to tend to think or behave in a particular way" (primary); Noun: "a slope" (secondary entry) | Our def uses noun sense which is valid but Oxford's primary entry is the VERB meaning "to tend toward." The noun is a separate secondary entry. Not wrong but presents a less common usage first. |
| border | "a line between places" | "the line that divides two countries or areas; the land near this line" | Our def is oversimplified. Oxford specifies it's between countries/areas specifically. "Between places" is vague — a doorway is between places but isn't a border. Minor. |
| choice | "something you pick" | "an act of choosing between two or more possibilities; something that you can choose" | Our def conflates the result with the act. Oxford's primary sense is the ACT of choosing, not the thing chosen. Slightly misleading for learners. |
| banal | "boring because it has been said too many times" | "very ordinary and containing nothing that is interesting or important" | Our def implies repetition causes banality. Oxford says it's about being ordinary/uninteresting — not necessarily from overuse. The "said too many times" framing is closer to "cliché" than "banal." |
| profound (level 3a) | "very deep and having a lot of meaning" | Primary: "very great; felt or experienced very strongly" | Captures secondary not primary sense (see above) |
| lofty | "very high and impressive" | Primary: "(of buildings, mountains) very high and impressive"; also: "deserving praise for high moral quality" (approving); "showing belief you are worth more" (disapproving) | Our def matches the physical sense but misses that Oxford's prominent usage is often about ideals/ambitions being admirably high. Not wrong but incomplete. |
| penurious | "very poor; unwilling to spend money" | "very poor" (synonym: destitute, penniless) | Our def adds "unwilling to spend money" (miserly). Oxford does NOT include this meaning — that would be "parsimonious." Our def conflates two different words. This is a factual error. |
| acrimonious | "angry and bitter in tone" | "(of an argument, etc.) angry and full of strong bitter feelings and words" | Our def says "in tone" which suggests vocal quality. Oxford specifies it applies to arguments/disputes, not a person's tone of voice. Minor framing issue. |

## Notes

### Words that match well (examples)
- **puppy**: Ours "a baby dog" vs Oxford "a young dog" — essentially identical
- **ladybug**: Ours "a small red bug with black spots" vs Oxford "a small flying insect, usually red with black spots" — matches
- **discover**: Ours "to find or learn about something for the first time" vs Oxford "to be the first person to become aware that a particular place or thing exists" — very close
- **obsolete**: Ours "no longer useful because something better exists" vs Oxford "no longer used because something new has been invented" — matches
- **indefatigable**: Ours "never getting tired or giving up" vs Oxford "never giving up or getting tired of doing something" — nearly identical
- **eschew**: Ours "to choose to avoid or stay away from something" vs Oxford "to deliberately avoid or keep away from something" — matches
- **extol**: Ours "to praise something with excitement" vs Oxford "to praise somebody/something very much" — close (ours adds "excitement" which isn't in Oxford, but not misleading)

### Key Factual Error
**penurious** — Our definition includes "unwilling to spend money" which is NOT in Oxford's definition. Oxford only defines it as "very poor" (synonym: destitute). The miserly meaning belongs to words like "parsimonious" or "miserly." This should be corrected.

### Sense Priority Issues
**profound** and **incline** present secondary/less-common senses as if primary. For a vocabulary learning app, this could mislead students about how these words are most commonly used.
